# Strategic Analysis Frameworks

Frameworks for analyzing businesses, industries, and competitive dynamics.

## McKinsey 7S Framework

**Origin**: Developed by McKinsey consultants Tom Peters and Robert Waterman in the 1980s

**Purpose**: Analyze organizational effectiveness through 7 interconnected elements.

### The 7 Elements

```
                    ┌─────────────────┐
                    │  Shared Values  │  (Center - connects all)
                    └────────┬────────┘
                             │
        ┌────────────────────┼────────────────────┐
        │                    │                    │
   ┌────┴────┐         ┌─────┴─────┐        ┌────┴────┐
   │ Strategy │         │ Structure │        │ Systems │   HARD Elements
   └────┬────┘         └─────┬─────┘        └────┬────┘
        │                    │                    │
        └────────────────────┼────────────────────┘
                             │
        ┌────────────────────┼────────────────────┐
        │                    │                    │
   ┌────┴────┐         ┌─────┴─────┐        ┌────┴────┐
   │  Style  │         │   Staff   │        │ Skills  │   SOFT Elements
   └─────────┘         └───────────┘        └─────────┘
```

### Hard vs Soft Elements

| Hard Elements | Soft Elements |
|--------------|---------------|
| Easier to define | Harder to define |
| Tangible | Intangible |
| Can be directly managed | Influenced by culture |
| Strategy, Structure, Systems | Style, Staff, Skills, Shared Values |

### Detailed Element Analysis

#### 1. Strategy
- What is the organization's plan to achieve competitive advantage?
- How does it respond to market changes?
- Is it clearly articulated and understood?

**Questions**:
- What is our sustainable competitive advantage?
- How do we differentiate from competitors?
- How do we handle competitive threats?

#### 2. Structure
- How is the organization divided and coordinated?
- What is the reporting hierarchy?
- How do teams and divisions communicate?

**Questions**:
- Is the structure centralized or decentralized?
- How are decisions made?
- Are there clear lines of responsibility?

#### 3. Systems
- What are the daily processes and procedures?
- How is work done and information shared?
- What are the formal and informal processes?

**Questions**:
- What are the core processes?
- What systems support decision-making?
- How is performance measured and rewarded?

#### 4. Shared Values
- What are the core values and culture?
- What beliefs guide behavior?
- What is the organization's mission?

**Questions**:
- What values are truly practiced (not just stated)?
- How are values reinforced?
- Are values aligned with strategy?

#### 5. Style
- What is the leadership and management style?
- How do leaders behave and interact?
- What actions do leaders take?

**Questions**:
- Is leadership participative or directive?
- How is conflict handled?
- How are employees treated?

#### 6. Staff
- Who are the employees?
- What are their capabilities and motivations?
- How are they recruited, developed, retained?

**Questions**:
- Do we have the right people?
- How are people developed and promoted?
- What are the gaps in talent?

#### 7. Skills
- What distinctive capabilities exist?
- What competencies does the organization have?
- What can the organization do well?

**Questions**:
- What are our core competencies?
- Are there skill gaps for our strategy?
- How do we develop new capabilities?

### How to Use 7S

1. **Map current state** for each element
2. **Define desired state** for each element
3. **Identify gaps** between current and desired
4. **Analyze alignment** between elements
5. **Develop action plan** to close gaps and improve alignment

## Porter's Five Forces

**Origin**: Michael Porter, Harvard Business School, 1979

**Purpose**: Analyze industry structure and competitive intensity to assess profitability potential.

### The Five Forces

```
                    ┌─────────────────────┐
                    │   Threat of New     │
                    │      Entrants       │
                    └──────────┬──────────┘
                               │
                               ▼
┌─────────────────┐    ┌───────────────┐    ┌─────────────────┐
│   Bargaining    │    │   Industry    │    │   Bargaining    │
│   Power of      │◄───│   Rivalry     │───►│   Power of      │
│   Suppliers     │    │  (Center)     │    │   Buyers        │
└─────────────────┘    └───────┬───────┘    └─────────────────┘
                               │
                               ▼
                    ┌─────────────────────┐
                    │  Threat of          │
                    │  Substitutes        │
                    └─────────────────────┘
```

### Detailed Force Analysis

#### 1. Threat of New Entrants

**Barriers to entry**:
- Economies of scale
- Capital requirements
- Brand loyalty
- Switching costs
- Access to distribution
- Government regulations
- Proprietary technology

**High barrier = Low threat = Good for incumbents**

#### 2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers

**Suppliers are powerful when**:
- Few suppliers dominate
- No substitutes available
- Supplier's product is differentiated
- High switching costs
- Supplier can forward integrate

#### 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers

**Buyers are powerful when**:
- Few buyers, high volume
- Products are standardized
- Low switching costs
- Buyers can backward integrate
- Product is not important to buyer's quality

#### 4. Threat of Substitutes

**Substitutes are threatening when**:
- Price-performance ratio is attractive
- Switching costs are low
- Buyer propensity to substitute is high

**Note**: Substitutes are different from competitors - they fulfill the same need differently

#### 5. Industry Rivalry

**Rivalry is intense when**:
- Many competitors of similar size
- Slow industry growth
- High fixed costs
- Low differentiation
- High exit barriers
- Diverse competitors

### Scoring Template

| Force | Score (1-5) | Key Factors |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| New Entrants | | |
| Supplier Power | | |
| Buyer Power | | |
| Substitutes | | |
| Rivalry | | |
| **Overall** | | |

**Scoring**: 1 = Very Low, 5 = Very High (higher = worse for industry profitability)

## SWOT Analysis

**Origin**: Albert Humphrey, Stanford Research Institute, 1960s

**Purpose**: Evaluate internal strengths/weaknesses and external opportunities/threats.

### The SWOT Matrix

```
                    HELPFUL                     HARMFUL
                 (to objective)             (to objective)
              ┌───────────────────┬───────────────────┐
   INTERNAL   │                   │                   │
   (origin)   │    STRENGTHS      │    WEAKNESSES     │
              │             │             │
              │                   │                   │
              ├───────────────────┼───────────────────┤
   EXTERNAL   │                   │                   │
   (origin)   │   OPPORTUNITIES   │      THREATS      │
              │             │             │
              │                   │                   │
              └───────────────────┴───────────────────┘
```

### How to Conduct SWOT

#### Step 1: Gather Information
- Internal: Financial reports, employee feedback, process audits
- External: Market research, competitor analysis, trend reports

#### Step 2: Identify Factors

**Strengths** (What do we do well?):
- Core competencies
- Unique resources or assets
- Competitive advantages
- Strong brand or reputation

**Weaknesses** (Where do we fall short?):
- Skill gaps
- Resource limitations
- Poor processes
- Weak brand areas

**Opportunities** (What external trends favor us?):
- Market growth
- Technology changes
- Regulatory changes
- Competitor weaknesses

**Threats** (What external factors could harm us?):
- New competitors
- Changing customer preferences
- Economic downturn
- Regulatory challenges

#### Step 3: Develop Strategies (TOWS Matrix)

| | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|
| **Opportunities** | **SO**: Use strengths to capture opportunities | **WO**: Overcome weaknesses by pursuing opportunities |
| **Threats** | **ST**: Use strengths to avoid threats | **WT**: Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats |

## BCG Matrix

**Origin**: Boston Consulting Group, 1970s

**Purpose**: Portfolio analysis to guide resource allocation across business units or products.

### The Matrix

```
                         MARKET SHARE
                    High              Low
               ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
         High  │      STARS      │   QUESTION      │
               │       ★         │    MARKS ?      │
    MARKET     │                 │                 │
    GROWTH     │  Invest heavily │ Invest or divest│
               ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
         Low   │   CASH COWS     │      DOGS       │
               │       $         │       ✗         │
               │                 │                 │
               │ Milk for cash   │ Divest or hold  │
               └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

### Category Strategies

| Category | Characteristics | Strategy |
|----------|----------------|----------|
| **Stars** | High growth, High share | Invest to maintain leadership |
| **Cash Cows** | Low growth, High share | Harvest profits, minimal investment |
| **Question Marks** | High growth, Low share | Invest to grow or divest |
| **Dogs** | Low growth, Low share | Divest or manage for cash |

### Ideal Portfolio Flow

```
Question Mark → Star → Cash Cow → (Fund new Question Marks)
                                         ↓
                                    Eventually retire
```

## 3C Framework

**Origin**: Kenichi Ohmae, McKinsey Japan, 1982

**Purpose**: Analyze strategic position through three key factors.

### The Three Cs

```
              CUSTOMER
             (Customer)
                △
               / \
              /   \
             /     \
            /       \
           /         \
    COMPANY ───────── COMPETITOR
    (Company)            (Competitor)
```

### Analysis Questions

#### Customer
- Who are our target customers?
- What are their needs and preferences?
- How do they make purchase decisions?
- What do they value most?
- How are their needs changing?

#### Competitor
- Who are our main competitors?
- What are their strengths and weaknesses?
- What is their strategy?
- How might they respond to our actions?
- Who might enter the market?

#### Company
- What are our core competencies?
- What resources do we have?
- What is our cost position?
- What is our brand strength?
- What are our weaknesses?

### Strategic Implications

The goal is to find a position where:
- **Company** capabilities match **Customer** needs
- Better than **Competitors** can deliver
- Sustainable over time

## 4P Marketing Mix

**Origin**: E. Jerome McCarthy, 1960

**Purpose**: Tactical marketing framework for product/service positioning.

### The Four Ps

| Element | Key Questions |
|---------|---------------|
| **Product** | What are we selling? Features? Quality? Branding? |
| **Price** | What do we charge? Discounts? Payment terms? |
| **Place** | Where do customers buy? Distribution channels? |
| **Promotion** | How do we communicate? Advertising? Sales? |

### Extended: 7P for Services

| Additional Ps | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| **People** | Service staff quality and training |
| **Process** | Service delivery procedures |
| **Physical Evidence** | Tangible elements of service |

## Value Chain Analysis

**Origin**: Michael Porter, 1985

**Purpose**: Identify activities that create value and competitive advantage.

### Primary Activities

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                         MARGIN                                    │
├────────────┬────────────┬────────────┬────────────┬─────────────┤
│  Inbound   │            │  Outbound  │ Marketing  │   Service   │
│  Logistics │ Operations │  Logistics │  & Sales   │             │
├────────────┴────────────┴────────────┴────────────┴─────────────┤
│                    PRIMARY ACTIVITIES                             │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Support Activities

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                 Firm Infrastructure                               │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│               Human Resource Management                           │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│               Technology Development                              │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                    Procurement                                    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                      SUPPORT ACTIVITIES
```

### How to Use

1. **Map all activities** in your value chain
2. **Identify cost drivers** for each activity
3. **Identify value drivers** for each activity
4. **Compare to competitors** where possible
5. **Find opportunities** to reduce cost or increase value

## PESTEL Analysis

**Origin**: Francis Aguilar (as ETPS), 1967; expanded over time

**Purpose**: Analyze macro-environmental factors affecting an organization.

### The Six Factors

| Factor | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **P**olitical | Government policies, stability, trade | Tax policy, trade tariffs, political stability |
| **E**conomic | Economic conditions and trends | GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, unemployment |
| **S**ocial | Societal trends and demographics | Population growth, age distribution, lifestyle changes |
| **T**echnological | Technology changes and innovation | R&D activity, automation, digital transformation |
| **E**nvironmental | Ecological and environmental factors | Climate change, sustainability, environmental regulations |
| **L**egal | Laws and regulations | Employment law, consumer protection, industry regulations |

### PESTEL Analysis Template

| Factor | Current Trends | Impact (H/M/L) | Opportunity or Threat? |
|--------|---------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Political | | | |
| Economic | | | |
| Social | | | |
| Technological | | | |
| Environmental | | | |
| Legal | | | |

### PESTEL + SWOT Integration

```
PESTEL (External Macro)     →     SWOT (O & T)
Porter's Five Forces (Industry)  →     SWOT (O & T)
Internal Analysis            →     SWOT (S & W)
```

## Ansoff Matrix

**Origin**: Igor Ansoff, 1957

**Purpose**: Identify growth strategies based on products and markets.

### The Matrix

```
                           PRODUCTS
                    Existing          New
              ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
    Existing  │     MARKET      │    PRODUCT      │
              │   PENETRATION   │   DEVELOPMENT   │
   MARKETS    │                 │                 │
              │   Lowest Risk   │   Medium Risk   │
              ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
      New     │     MARKET      │ DIVERSIFICATION │
              │   DEVELOPMENT   │                 │
              │                 │                 │
              │   Medium Risk   │   Highest Risk  │
              └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

### Strategy Details

| Strategy | Description | Tactics | Risk Level |
|----------|-------------|---------|------------|
| **Market Penetration** | Sell more existing products to existing markets | Increase marketing, lower prices, loyalty programs | Low |
| **Product Development** | New products for existing markets | R&D, product extensions, new features | Medium |
| **Market Development** | Existing products to new markets | New geographies, new segments, new channels | Medium |
| **Diversification** | New products for new markets | Acquisitions, new business lines | High |

### Diversification Types

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Related (Concentric)** | New business related to core | Apple: computers → phones |
| **Unrelated (Conglomerate)** | New business unrelated to core | Virgin: music → airlines |
| **Horizontal** | New products for same customers | McDonald's: burgers → salads |
| **Vertical** | Move up/down value chain | Netflix: distribution → production |

## GE-McKinsey Matrix

**Origin**: McKinsey for General Electric, 1970s

**Purpose**: Portfolio prioritization based on industry attractiveness and competitive strength.

### The Matrix

```
                        COMPETITIVE STRENGTH
                    Strong    Medium     Weak
              ┌──────────┬──────────┬──────────┐
      High    │  INVEST  │  INVEST  │ PROTECT  │
              │  /GROW   │ SELECTIVE│          │
              ├──────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
INDUSTRY Medium│  INVEST  │ SELECTIVE│  HARVEST │
ATTRACTIVE-   │ SELECTIVE│          │          │
NESS          ├──────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
      Low     │ PROTECT  │  HARVEST │  DIVEST  │
              │          │          │          │
              └──────────┴──────────┴──────────┘
```

### Scoring Factors

**Industry Attractiveness**:
- Market size and growth rate
- Profit margins
- Competitive intensity
- Technology requirements
- Environmental/social factors
- Barriers to entry

**Competitive Strength**:
- Market share
- Brand strength
- Production capacity
- Profit margins relative to competitors
- Technological capability
- Management quality

### GE-McKinsey vs BCG Matrix

| Aspect | BCG Matrix | GE-McKinsey |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Dimensions | 2 (growth, share) | Multiple factors per axis |
| Complexity | Simple | More comprehensive |
| Subjectivity | Low | Higher (weighted scores) |
| Best for | Quick portfolio view | Detailed strategic planning |

## Blue Ocean Strategy

**Origin**: W. Chan Kim & Renée Mauborgne, 2005

**Purpose**: Create uncontested market space instead of competing in existing markets.

### Red Ocean vs Blue Ocean

| Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|-----------|------------|
| Compete in existing market | Create uncontested market space |
| Beat the competition | Make competition irrelevant |
| Exploit existing demand | Create and capture new demand |
| Value-cost trade-off | Break value-cost trade-off |
| Differentiate OR low cost | Differentiate AND low cost |

### Strategy Canvas

```
Value Curve Comparison

High │         ●─────●
     │        /       \        ○ Your new offering
     │   ●───●         ●      ● Industry standard
     │  /               \
Low  │ ●                 ●───●
     └─────────────────────────────
       Factor Factor Factor Factor
         A      B      C      D
```

### Four Actions Framework (ERRC)

| Action | Question | Purpose |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **E**liminate | Which factors should be eliminated? | Remove costly factors with little value |
| **R**educe | Which factors should be reduced below standard? | Cut over-designed elements |
| **R**aise | Which factors should be raised above standard? | Address under-served needs |
| **C**reate | Which factors should be created that never existed? | Offer unprecedented value |

### Six Paths Framework

Look across:
1. **Alternative industries** - not just competitors
2. **Strategic groups** - different tiers within industry
3. **Buyer groups** - purchasers, users, influencers
4. **Complementary offerings** - what happens before/during/after
5. **Functional-emotional orientation** - switch the appeal
6. **Time** - trends that affect value

## VRIO Framework

**Origin**: Jay Barney, 1991 (Resource-Based View)

**Purpose**: Assess if resources/capabilities provide sustainable competitive advantage.

### The VRIO Questions

| Criterion | Question | If No... |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **V**aluable | Does it help exploit opportunities or neutralize threats? | Competitive disadvantage |
| **R**are | Is it scarce among competitors? | Competitive parity |
| **I**mitable | Is it costly for others to imitate? | Temporary advantage |
| **O**rganized | Is the firm organized to capture value? | Unrealized advantage |

### VRIO Analysis Flow

```
Is the resource VALUABLE?
        │
        ├── No → Competitive Disadvantage
        │
        └── Yes → Is it RARE?
                    │
                    ├── No → Competitive Parity
                    │
                    └── Yes → Is it hard to IMITATE?
                                │
                                ├── No → Temporary Advantage
                                │
                                └── Yes → Is firm ORGANIZED?
                                            │
                                            ├── No → Unrealized Advantage
                                            │
                                            └── Yes → SUSTAINED ADVANTAGE
```

### VRIO Assessment Template

| Resource/Capability | V | R | I | O | Competitive Implication |
|---------------------|---|---|---|---|-------------------------|
| Brand reputation | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | Sustained advantage |
| Patents | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ? | Temporary/unrealized |
| Distribution network | ✓ | ✓ | ✗ | ✓ | Temporary advantage |
| Skilled workforce | ✓ | ✗ | - | - | Competitive parity |

### Barriers to Imitation

| Barrier | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Path dependency** | Built over time, can't be bought |
| **Causal ambiguity** | Competitors don't know what to imitate |
| **Social complexity** | Culture, relationships, trust |
| **Patents/legal** | Legal protection |

## Porter's Generic Strategies

**Origin**: Michael Porter, 1980

**Purpose**: Achieve competitive advantage through strategic positioning.

### The Three Strategies

```
                         COMPETITIVE ADVANTAGE
                    Lower Cost    Differentiation
              ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
    Broad     │      COST       │ DIFFERENTIATION │
   (Industry) │   LEADERSHIP    │                 │
              │                 │                 │
COMPETITIVE   ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
SCOPE         │                 │                 │
    Narrow    │   COST FOCUS    │ DIFFERENTIATION │
   (Segment)  │                 │     FOCUS       │
              │                 │                 │
              └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

### Strategy Details

| Strategy | How to Win | Requirements | Risks |
|----------|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **Cost Leadership** | Lowest cost producer | Scale, efficiency, tight cost control | Technology changes, imitation |
| **Differentiation** | Unique value customers will pay for | Innovation, marketing, quality | Customers may not pay premium |
| **Focus (Cost)** | Lowest cost in niche segment | Deep segment knowledge | Segment may shrink |
| **Focus (Differentiation)** | Unique value in niche segment | Specialization | Broader competitors enter niche |

### Stuck in the Middle

Companies that fail to pursue a clear strategy get "stuck in the middle":
- Neither lowest cost nor differentiated
- Below-average profitability
- Unclear value proposition

### Strategy Alignment

| Strategy | Value Chain Focus | Organization | Culture |
|----------|------------------|--------------|---------|
| Cost Leadership | Operations, procurement | Centralized, efficiency | Cost-conscious |
| Differentiation | R&D, marketing | Decentralized, creative | Innovation-driven |
| Focus | Customer intimacy | Specialized | Customer-obsessed |
